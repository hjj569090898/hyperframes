# Hook Models

Choose one hook before writing scenes. The hook controls the first scene copy, motion intensity, and asset choice.

## Result-First

Open with the outcome the buyer wants.

Use when the product has a measurable promise: faster launches, more leads, lower churn, cleaner reporting.

Template: "Launch-ready videos from one URL."

## Pain Question

Name the frustrating moment directly.

Use when the audience already feels the problem and needs recognition.

Template: "Still turning product pages into decks by hand?"

## Contrast

Place the old workflow against the new workflow.

Use when the product replaces a messy manual process.

Template: "Before: three tools. After: one campaign-ready video."

## Challenge

Make a bold claim that asks the viewer to test it.

Use when speed, automation, or quality is the key differentiator.

Template: "Give us a URL. Get a launch video."

## Mistake

Call out a common wrong behavior.

Use when buyers are wasting effort on a familiar but weak workflow.

Template: "Your landing page is doing too much work alone."

## Proof Tease

Open with a metric or credibility marker, then explain.

Use when there is real evidence in the brief or asset inventory.

Template: "Teams cut launch content time by 80%."

## Selection Rule

Prefer the sharpest honest hook. Do not invent metrics, customers, or awards. If evidence is weak, use pain, contrast, or result-first instead of proof.
